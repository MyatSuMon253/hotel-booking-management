### Abstract

Rangoon Heritage Hotel is a vintage-style hotel established in 1980 that has grown from a small operation of 15 rooms and 9 staff into an organization with 50 rooms and 27 staff. Despite this growth, the operational processes for room management, reservations, check-in/check-out, payment handling, refunds, and customer record-keeping have remained largely paper-based. Paper records, ledgers, and manual workflows create delays, increase the likelihood of human error, complicate reporting, and introduce data security risks through loss, theft, or damage.

This report documents the analysis, design, implementation, and evaluation of a web-based Hotel Booking Management System intended to modernize those workflows while preserving the hotel’s heritage-focused service identity. The proposed solution centralizes operational data in a database, automates high-frequency tasks (availability updates, booking confirmation, invoicing), enforces role-based access control to protect sensitive information, and supports online payments through Stripe with reliable server-side confirmation via webhook processing. Additionally, the system extends customer engagement through membership tiers, referral rewards, reviews, and buffet dinner bookings as an auxiliary revenue stream. The outcome is a full-stack system composed of a React (Vite) client and an Express/Apollo GraphQL server backed by MongoDB, designed to reduce processing time, improve accuracy, and provide actionable reporting for management decisions.

## 1. Introduction of Current Business

### 1.1 Background

Rangoon Heritage Hotel was started in 1980. It is a vintage-style hotel that highlights the culture, heritage, and art of Myanmar. It began as a small business with 9 staff and 15 rooms. Over time, it has grown into a hotel with 27 staff and 50 rooms. The hotel is recognized for its traditional atmosphere and the quality of its customer service. However, the internal operational approach has not evolved alongside the business scale. Many administrative activities still rely on paper documents, physical filing, and handwritten ledgers.

As the number of rooms, staff, and guest interactions increases, the reliance on paper becomes increasingly costly. The organization experiences slower service processes, more opportunities for data inconsistency (especially between departments), reduced visibility into performance metrics, and higher exposure to security incidents such as document loss, unauthorized access, or disaster damage. These factors directly impact guest satisfaction and can constrain the hotel’s future growth.

From an information systems perspective, the hotel’s current state can be described as a low-integration operating model. Each department (front desk, finance, management, and service teams) maintains its own records and relies on manual handoffs to coordinate. In such environments, the primary operational risk is not only the existence of incorrect data but also the inability to quickly detect incorrect data. Paper systems have limited built-in validation (e.g., preventing double booking) and limited traceability (e.g., identifying who changed a record and when). As the hotel continues to expand its business activities, digital support becomes a strategic requirement rather than an optional improvement.

The decision to build a web-based system is motivated by several practical constraints. A web system can be accessed from common devices without dedicated client installations, supports rapid iteration, and can be deployed centrally. The web model fits both guest-facing scenarios (customers browsing and booking from personal devices) and staff workflows (admin portals and dashboards accessible from desktops). Additionally, a web architecture can integrate with external services for payments, media storage, and email notifications, which would be difficult to manage reliably in purely manual operations.

### 1.2 Current Business Operations (As-Is)

The current operation can be summarized as a collection of department-specific tasks that require manual coordination:

1. Managing rooms: Room information is recorded on paper. Updates require physically locating the room’s records and modifying them by hand. In a growing hotel, this quickly becomes slow and error-prone.
2. Handling bookings: Staff check room availability on a paper chart, then complete booking forms manually. Copies are stored in physical files. Booking changes and cancellations require locating the original paper record and marking it as cancelled, then manually updating charts.
3. Managing check-in and check-out: Guests fill check-in forms manually at the front desk. At checkout, staff manually sum charges and prepare payment details.
4. Processing payments and refunds: Payments are recorded in a ledger book. Refunds require manual approval procedures and manual record adjustments.
5. Managing customer information: Guest information is stored in paper files. Reuse of customer data or retrieval of history depends on physical file access and is difficult to integrate into service personalization.

In practice, these activities also imply hidden operational costs. For example, searching for the correct paper form, ensuring that multiple copies of the same record are updated consistently, and correcting mistakes after they have propagated through multiple ledgers are all time-consuming. These costs tend to be underestimated because they are distributed across employees and are not accounted as explicit financial expenses. In digital transformation projects, making these “invisible costs” explicit is important because it explains why modernization yields immediate business benefits even if the hotel’s outward service has remained strong.

### 1.3 Current Business Issues (Problem Statement)

The paper-based approach generates systemic issues that scale non-linearly with business growth:

1. Delay time: Manual tasks take longer, especially at peak hours. Information may not be updated promptly across departments, leading to decisions based on outdated data.
2. Data security: Paper files are vulnerable to theft, loss, tampering, and damage. There is no centralized access control, audit trail, or systematic backup strategy for physical records.
3. Difficulty in reporting: Financial metrics, ROI, occupancy rates, and performance indicators (KPIs) are manually calculated. Reports tend to be numeric and difficult to visualize. Management decisions are therefore slower and less data-driven.

The operational issues can be organized into three interconnected categories:

1. Process inefficiency: time delays and duplicated effort across departments.
2. Information quality risk: inconsistencies, missing records, and calculation mistakes.
3. Governance and assurance gaps: weak access control, limited traceability, and limited disaster resilience.

These categories matter because they reinforce each other. For example, process inefficiency increases staff pressure, which increases error risk, which then increases time spent on correction. Similarly, governance gaps make it difficult to trust data used for management decisions, reducing the value of reporting. A successful information system must therefore address the full loop: workflow speed, data correctness, and controlled access.

### 1.4 Improvements for the Current Business (To-Be Vision)

To address the identified issues, the key improvements required are:

1. Centralized database: Store operational data in a single system-of-record so all departments access the same up-to-date information.
2. Automation: Automate repetitive operations such as booking confirmation, availability updates, invoice creation, and status tracking.
3. Secure data access: Use authentication and authorization to protect sensitive guest and financial data. Centralized digital storage is also more resilient against disasters compared with paper.
4. Automatic report generation: Generate revenue and operational reports automatically, including charts and dashboards that improve decision-making.

The improvement goals can be evaluated as measurable outcomes:

- Reduced booking processing time (minutes to seconds).
- Reduced rate of booking conflicts (double booking events reduced toward zero).
- Reduced time to generate monthly operational reports (hours/days to minutes).
- Improved data security posture (role-based access and reduced exposure of sensitive information).
- Improved guest experience (faster confirmation, clearer payment status, and self-service capabilities).

While some benefits are directly measurable, others (such as improved trust and brand perception) must be evaluated qualitatively through customer feedback and staff experience. Both categories are addressed in the evaluation chapter.

### 1.5 Proposed System Scope

The proposed system is a web-based solution designed to computerize core hotel operations and streamline daily routines. The system’s core is a central database that stores guest, room, booking, payment, and operational records. The design supports key hotel roles and workflows and includes:

- Room management: Inventory of 50 rooms with availability status, pricing, and images.
- Booking management: Real-time reservation tracking that blocks rooms once reserved and prevents double booking.
- Check-in/check-out support: Workflow support through pre-filled forms based on booking data and automated invoice generation.
- Payment and refund management: Online payment processing and refund support for eligible cancellations.
- Customer management: Profiles with booking history, membership tiers, referral codes, and points to support personalized service.
- Analytics and reporting: Dashboard-level metrics and charts for occupancy, sales, booking status, and payment methods.

This scope intentionally focuses on core business value for a mid-sized hotel. It does not attempt to replicate the full feature set of large enterprise PMS platforms such as full channel management (OTA synchronization), housekeeping scheduling, maintenance ticketing, or multi-property support. Those features are potentially valuable, but introducing them prematurely would increase complexity and risk for adoption. Instead, the project adopts an incremental approach: deliver a strong operational foundation first, then extend into adjacent modules as the organization matures in digital operations.

## 2. Aims

The system’s aim is to process hotel daily operations more efficiently, boost net profit, and minimize human errors while enabling personalized service that honors the hotel’s heritage identity. This aim is both operational (reducing time and errors) and strategic (improving business outcomes through better reporting, loyalty programs, and revenue channels).

Operational aims include reducing front desk waiting time, preventing booking conflicts, and improving data accuracy. Strategic aims include increasing conversion through better room presentation and easier payment, improving retention through membership and referral features, and supporting management decisions through dashboards and reporting.

To make aims assessable, they can be translated into evaluation questions:

1. Efficiency: How much time is saved for key tasks such as availability checking, booking creation, and report generation?
2. Accuracy: Does the system reduce booking overlap incidents and billing calculation errors compared to paper-based processes?
3. Security: Does the system restrict sensitive operations to authorized roles and reduce the risk of data exposure?
4. Business outcomes: Does the system enable improved revenue visibility and support actions that improve occupancy or retention?

These questions guide both the design choices (e.g., overlap prevention and webhook confirmation) and the evaluation strategy (e.g., time measurements, scenario testing, and dashboard metric validation).

### 3.1 Report on Research (07 July 2025 – 18 Aug 2025)

The research phase collects, examines, and records requirements of current hotel operations through interviews and surveys. The goal is to capture both functional needs (what the system must do) and non-functional requirements (how well it must do it).

Key research activities and outputs:

1. Conduct interviews with front desk staff to identify pain points such as overbooking risk and long waiting time.
2. Collect requirements through stakeholder interviews and surveys (front office, management, finance, HR, and guest-facing staff).
3. Identify key stakeholders and compile required features.
4. Examine market trends and customer behavior (e.g., expectation of online booking, mobile-friendly experiences).
5. Competitor analysis: review similar hotel management and booking systems.
6. Prepare Business Requirements Document (BRD).
7. Identify risks and plan risk management.
8. Perform feasibility study (technical, operational, economic).
9. Produce a research report summarizing results and conclusions.

#### 3.1.1 Research Approach and Methods

For a hotel operations project, requirements research benefits from a mixed-method approach:

1. Semi-structured interviews: Interviews with staff capture deep context about current workflows, exceptions, and pain points. Semi-structured formats allow consistent topic coverage while leaving room for unexpected insights.
2. Process observation: Observing check-in and booking workflows at peak times reveals friction points that staff might normalize and not explicitly report.
3. Document review: Paper booking forms, ledger templates, and policy documents help define required data fields, validation rules, and compliance needs.
4. Surveys: Surveys can quantify satisfaction with current workflows and prioritize problems by frequency and impact.

In this project, the objective is to build a system that matches operational reality. Therefore, the research must not only gather “what the system should do” but also capture “what goes wrong today” and “what exceptions the system must handle.” For example, cancellations are not a simple delete; they are a business event that requires status changes, reasons, potential refunds, and reporting implications.

#### 3.1.2 Stakeholder Mapping

Stakeholders should be mapped by influence and impact:

- High influence / high impact: management, front desk lead, finance lead.
- High impact / lower influence: customers, junior front desk staff.
- Operational support: IT/developers, external services (payment, email, media).

Mapping helps identify who must approve design decisions and who must be included in usability evaluation. It also clarifies the ethical duty to consider less influential roles (e.g., staff whose work is heavily affected by automation).

#### 3.1.3 Requirements Elicitation Outputs

Key outputs include:

- Persona definitions: customer persona (tourist, business traveler), staff persona (front desk, admin).
- Use cases and user stories: to capture task-level requirements.
- Prioritization artifacts: MoSCoW categorization and timeboxing.
- Risk register: identified risks, probability, impact, mitigation strategy.
- Feasibility summary: technical and operational viability.

These outputs support traceability from business problem to design and implementation.

### 3.2 Service Processes

The current service processes are fragmented and department-based rather than system-integrated. A computerized system should reduce handoffs and improve “single source of truth” access. In a hotel context, service processes are time-sensitive; any delay at check-in/check-out can directly affect guest satisfaction. Therefore, the system is designed to support a streamlined workflow:

1. Customers browse availability and make bookings without staff needing to manually check charts.
2. Staff can immediately see bookings and availability updates.
3. Payment confirmation occurs through integrated processing, reducing manual ledger work.
4. Management dashboards summarize performance metrics for timely decisions.

#### 3.2.1 As-Is vs To-Be Process Mapping

Academic documentation often includes a conceptual “As-Is” and “To-Be” mapping:

As-Is (paper):

- Staff checks a paper chart, writes a booking form, stores copies.
- Changes require searching physical files and updating multiple ledgers.
- Payment records are written by hand; confirmation depends on staff accuracy.
- Reporting is manual and time-consuming.

To-Be (system):

- System displays room listings and availability to customers and staff.
- Booking creation enforces validation and blocks overlapping dates.
- Payment confirmation is automated via a webhook-based integration.
- Reporting is generated from database records and presented in charts.

This mapping provides a clear justification for design features such as overlap detection, centralized storage, and dashboard aggregation.

### 3.3 Strength and Weakness (SWOT)

SWOT analysis identifies business and operational strengths and weaknesses of the current system:

Strengths:

- Stable for simple isolated workflows (low technical failure).
- Familiar to staff, requiring no training for paper processes.
- Easy to operate without electricity or network availability.

Weaknesses:

- Poor scalability: as bookings increase, paperwork increases linearly or worse.
- High error rate: manual calculations and duplication across files.
- Low data visibility: reporting requires manual aggregation, delaying insight.
- Weak security: no authentication, no audit trail, high physical vulnerability.

Opportunities:

- Digital transformation can increase guest satisfaction and conversion rates.
- Online payment reduces friction and supports international guests.
- Membership tiers and referral codes encourage retention and acquisition.
- Data-driven marketing through promotions and analytics.

Threats:

- Implementation risk: poor adoption if staff training is inadequate.
- Security threats: digital systems must resist hacking attempts.
- Operational reliance: system downtime could disrupt operations if not mitigated.
- Regulatory compliance: handling personal data and payments has legal implications.

#### 3.3.1 Implications of SWOT for System Design

SWOT is valuable when it informs concrete design decisions. Examples:

- Weakness: double booking risk → Design decision: overlap detection in booking creation.
- Weakness: slow reporting → Design decision: admin dashboard backed by database aggregation.
- Threat: security risk → Design decision: backend-enforced RBAC using `graphql-shield`, cookie-based authentication, and limited exposure of sensitive data.
- Opportunity: loyalty features → Design decision: membership tiers, referral codes, and referral points stored on user records and integrated into booking discounts.

### 3.5 Non-Functional Requirements

Non-functional requirements define measurable quality attributes:

Performance:

- Typical GraphQL queries should respond within an acceptable time under normal load.
- Booking creation must remain fast and must prevent race conditions and overlaps.

Security:

- Authentication is required for protected operations.
- Role-based authorization protects admin actions.
- Sensitive tokens should not be exposed to client scripts.

Scalability:

- Room listing supports pagination.
- Reporting uses database aggregation for performance.
- The architecture allows separation of client and server deployment.

Usability:

- Clear navigation and route separation between customer and admin portals.
- Form validation and error messages.
- Support for fast workflows during peak hours.

Availability and reliability:

- Server stability and database durability.
- Backup and recovery planning (supported through deployment and backup procedures).

Maintainability:

- Modular backend controllers and GraphQL resolvers.
- Clear client-side component separation and typed GraphQL operations.

#### 3.5.1 Measurement Criteria for NFRs

To keep NFRs actionable, each should have a measurement approach:

- Performance: measure median and p95 response time for key GraphQL operations under test conditions.
- Availability: define acceptable downtime windows and recovery procedures.
- Security: demonstrate that restricted operations fail for unauthorized roles, and that payment confirmation is based on signed webhook events.
- Usability: apply a questionnaire (e.g., SUS-style scoring) and task completion timing.
- Maintainability: code organization, modular boundaries, and consistent typing.

Even if a project does not implement automated measurements for all NFRs, defining measurement methods increases professional quality and supports future evaluation.

#### 3.10.1 Return on Investment (ROI)

ROI analysis for the system compares costs (development, deployment, training, maintenance) with benefits (labor time saved, increased conversion, reduced revenue loss from overbooking, higher retention).

Because actual financial values vary by hotel and season, this report provides an ROI method and a sample calculation using transparent assumptions:

1. Identify current labor hours spent on booking processing, ledger updates, and report generation.
2. Estimate time reductions per task after automation.
3. Translate hours saved into cost savings using staff hourly rates.
4. Estimate increased revenue from reduced booking friction and increased occupancy.
5. Estimate reduced losses from overbooking and billing errors.
6. Compare total annual benefits to total system cost.

Example assumptions (illustrative):

- Booking processing time reduction: from 10 minutes to 3 minutes per booking.
- Average bookings per day: 20 (seasonal variation).
- Staff cost: $4/hour equivalent (example; replace with actual payroll).
- Time saved per day: 20 bookings * 7 minutes = 140 minutes = 2.33 hours.
- Annual staff time saved: 2.33 hours/day * 365 ≈ 850 hours/year.
- Annual savings: 850 * $4 = $3,400/year.

This estimate excludes revenue increases and error reductions. In practice, the ROI improves significantly when considering revenue effects.

##### ROI Extension: Revenue and Loss Avoidance (Illustrative)

Operational cost savings are usually only a portion of total ROI. Additional value includes:

1. Conversion uplift: smoother browsing and payment can convert a portion of visitors into bookings that would otherwise be lost.
2. Reduced revenue leakage: double booking incidents, incorrect billing, and missed payments can cause direct losses or compensation costs.
3. Better pricing decisions: dashboards enable management to identify demand patterns and adjust prices or promotions strategically.

Illustrative conversion uplift example:

- Assume 500 monthly website visitors explore rooms.
- If the improved workflow increases conversion by 1% (from 4% to 5%), that is 5 additional bookings per month.
- If average booking value is $120, monthly uplift is $600, annual uplift is $7,200.

These values are examples; the key academic contribution is the method and the identification of measurable drivers, which management can replace with real data.

### 3.11 Risks from Implementation and After Implementation

Key risks:

1. Adoption risk: staff may resist change; mitigation includes training and phased rollout.
2. Data migration risk: incorrect mapping of paper records into the database; mitigation includes validation and staged migration.
3. Security risk: unauthorized access attempts; mitigation includes RBAC, secure cookies, and operational monitoring.

### 3.12 Feasibility Studies

#### 3.12.1 Break Even Cost

Break-even analysis estimates when the benefits equal the cost:

1. Calculate one-time costs (development, setup, training).
2. Calculate recurring costs (hosting, maintenance).
3. Estimate monthly benefit (time saved + revenue lift + error avoidance).
4. Break-even month = total cost / monthly benefit.

This approach encourages management to collect real operational data and update assumptions.

#### 3.12.2 Operational Feasibility

Operational feasibility assesses whether the system fits day-to-day work:

- Users can complete tasks with fewer steps than paper processes.
- Role-based access matches staff responsibilities.
- The UI is designed for speed and clarity, especially for booking workflows.
- Workflows for cancellations, payment checks, and invoice access reduce manual overhead.

#### 3.12.3 Technical Feasibility

Technical feasibility compares methodologies and technologies:

Methodologies:

- Waterfall: predictable but rigid; less suited for evolving requirements.
- Agile/Scrum: iterative delivery, frequent feedback, and adaptation.

This project’s structure aligns with iterative delivery: core modules first (auth, rooms, bookings) followed by payments, admin dashboards, and additional features (buffets, membership).

Database choice:

- MongoDB: flexible schema, rapid iteration, and good fit for document-oriented records like bookings and embedded sub-documents (customer, amount).
- Postgres: strong relational integrity and complex SQL capabilities; often used for highly structured financial systems.

For this system, MongoDB is technically feasible due to the clear entity boundaries and the advantage of rapid development. The design still models relationships using references (ObjectIds) for user-room-booking relationships.

Programming language choice:

- JavaScript/TypeScript: strong ecosystem for full-stack web development, consistent language across client and server, and strong tooling.
- Java/Python: strong ecosystems but would introduce different runtime/tooling and potentially slower integration for a React client.

The project uses TypeScript for both frontend and backend to improve maintainability through types.

##### Tooling Feasibility and Team Capability

In addition to technology selection, feasibility includes the team’s ability to operate and maintain the system:

- Availability of developers with JavaScript/TypeScript skills.
- Availability of operational knowledge to configure environment variables and deploy services.
- Ease of hiring or training staff for future enhancements.

This matters because a technically strong system that cannot be maintained becomes a long-term business risk. The project’s choice of widely adopted tooling improves staffing feasibility.

## 4. Legal, Ethical, Social and Professional Issues (LESP)

This chapter expands LESP issues for the project.

### 4.1 Legal Issues

Legal considerations apply to both personal data and financial data:

1. Data protection and privacy: guest names, emails, booking history, and potentially identification-related information must be protected. The system should limit access based on roles and avoid exposing sensitive data unnecessarily.
2. Payment handling: payment security is essential. Using Stripe Checkout reduces direct handling of card data, aligning with best practices for PCI scope reduction.
3. Record retention: financial records and invoices may be subject to local legal requirements for retention and reporting. The system’s database design supports consistent record keeping.
4. Licensing: third-party libraries and services should be used under compliant licenses, and production use should respect terms of service for external services.

### 4.2 Ethical Issues

Ethical responsibilities include:

- Confidentiality: hotel staff and developers must treat guest information confidentially.
- Integrity: prevent fraud, manipulation of bookings, or unauthorized discounts.

## 6. System Design and Architecture

### 6.1 High-Level Architecture

The system is a two-tier web application with external service integrations:

1. Client: React + TypeScript single-page application built with Vite. It uses React Router for navigation and Apollo Client for GraphQL communication. It includes separate layouts for customer and admin workflows.
2. Server: Express + Apollo Server GraphQL API. It handles authentication, authorization, business logic, and database access through Mongoose.
3. Database: MongoDB stores users, rooms, bookings, reviews, promotions, membership tiers, buffet dinners, and buffet bookings.
4. External services:
   - Stripe for online payments (checkout + webhook confirmation).
   - Cloudinary for media storage (avatars and room images).
   - SMTP provider for password reset emails.

The deployment-level view is represented in `_uml/deployment-diagram.puml`.

#### 6.1.1 Rationale for a Two-Workspace Architecture

Separating the solution into `client/` and `server/` provides pragmatic engineering and operational benefits:

1. Independent scaling and deployment: the React client can be deployed as static assets (CDN or static host), while the server can be scaled based on API demand and database load.
2. Reduced coupling: changes to UI screens do not require redeploying the API service and vice versa, as long as the GraphQL schema contract is maintained.
3. Security boundary: critical business rules and authorization checks live in the server. Even if a client is modified or an attacker crafts requests manually, server enforcement prevents unauthorized operations.
4. Better fault isolation: client build failures or UI issues do not necessarily crash the backend service. Similarly, backend incidents can be mitigated without rebuilding the client.

For a hotel context, this approach supports operational continuity. The front desk and admin portal must remain predictable, and maintenance activities should be able to target the affected component without forcing a full system redeployment.

### 6.2 API Design (GraphQL)

The project uses GraphQL because:

- It allows the client to query exactly the fields required for each view (room list, room details, dashboard metrics).
- It organizes operations into queries, mutations, and subscriptions, matching the project’s workflows.
- It supports typed schemas and structured input objects, which can be validated both in frontend schemas and backend resolver logic.

Primary GraphQL modules (typeDefs + resolvers):

- Rooms: listing with filters/pagination; room details; admin room CRUD and image deletion.
- Users: registration/login; currentUser/logout; profile update; password reset; admin user management; referral validation.
- Bookings: booking creation, payment update, cancellation; booking lists; booked dates; dashboard metrics; subscriptions.
- Payments: Stripe checkout session creation; webhook handler (REST).
- Reviews: review creation/update; review deletion; review eligibility check.
- Promotions: create/update/delete promotions; query promotions.
- Membership tiers: create/update/delete tiers; query tiers; ensure defaults.
- Buffets: dishes catalog queries; buffet dinner CRUD; buffet booking flows.

#### 6.2.1 Why GraphQL Fits This Project

GraphQL is appropriate for this application because the client screens require different shapes of data. The room list needs lightweight room summary data plus pagination metadata. The room detail view requires nested review objects and reviewer information. The admin dashboard requires aggregate metrics. With GraphQL, the client can request precisely the required fields for each screen rather than relying on rigid server-defined response shapes.

GraphQL also matches the project’s domain modules:

- Queries model “read” operations such as room listing, booking history, and dashboard metrics.
- Mutations model “write” operations such as creating bookings, confirming payments, cancellations, and admin CRUD operations.
- Subscriptions model real-time notifications for operational events.

The main engineering caution with GraphQL is ensuring that authorization is applied consistently because the API exposes many operations through one endpoint. This project mitigates that risk by applying `graphql-shield` rules at the schema level and by validating sensitive actions inside controllers.

### 6.3 Authentication and Authorization Design

Authentication design:

- Login issues a JWT stored in an HTTP-only cookie.
- Apollo server verifies the cookie on each request and attaches the authenticated user to GraphQL context.

Authorization design:

- `graphql-shield` defines rules for each operation.
- Admin-only operations are protected server-side, not only in the UI.

This approach reduces risk of unauthorized data access and ensures consistent enforcement.

#### 6.3.1 Threat Model (High Level)

The system must protect several categories of data and operations:

- Personal data: user identity, booking history, contact details.
- Financial data: payment status, amounts, refund records.
- Business assets: room inventory, pricing, promotions, and membership tiers.

Potential threat actors include unauthenticated users attempting to access protected data, authenticated customers attempting to access admin features, and external attackers targeting authentication or payment handling. The project’s design chooses mitigations that are realistic for the system size:

- Backend-enforced RBAC: admin actions are blocked unless the role contains `admin`.
- Cookie-based authentication: user identity is derived from server-verified JWT, not trusted client claims.
- Webhook-based payment confirmation: booking status is confirmed only after the server processes a signed payment event.

For production hardening, additional measures such as rate limiting, audit logging, and enhanced CSRF strategy are recommended, but the current implementation already addresses the most critical threats for a booking/payment system.

### 6.4 Data Model Design

MongoDB collections are used, with references for relationships:

- User ↔ Booking (user creates bookings)
- Room ↔ Booking (booking reserves a room)
- User ↔ Review ↔ Room (reviews connect users and rooms)
- BuffetDinner ↔ BuffetBooking (buffet bookings reference a dinner event)
- Promotions can reference applicable rooms; membership tiers define discount policies

The ER diagram is documented in `_uml/erd-diagram.puml`.

#### 6.4.1 Data Integrity Strategy in MongoDB

MongoDB provides flexibility, but integrity must be enforced through application design. This project uses:

- Mongoose schema validation for required fields and value constraints (e.g., membership tier enum, price validations, guestCount minimums).
- Server-side validation for cross-document rules such as booking overlap prevention and buffet capacity checks.
- Status-based logic to ensure that cancelled records do not reserve capacity and that confirmed/paid states are derived from trusted events.

This approach achieves the practical equivalent of relational integrity for the core workflows without requiring a relational database.

### 6.5 UI Design (Client Routing and Layout)

The client organizes routes around user type:

Public routes:

- `/`, `/rooms/:id`, `/attractions`, `/register`, `/login`, `/reset`, `/reset-password/:token`

Protected user routes:

- `/profile`, `/bookings`, payment/confirmation routes, `/invoice/:id`, buffet routes

Admin routes (protected with role=admin):

- `/admin/dashboard`, `/admin/rooms`, `/admin/bookings`, `/admin/customers`, `/admin/promotions`, buffet dinner management, membership tiers, reviews

The sitemap of routes is documented in `_uml/sitemap.puml`.

#### 6.5.1 UX Rationale for Customer vs Admin Separation

The customer UI is designed around conversion and clarity: browse rooms, understand details, book and pay, then manage bookings. The admin UI is designed as an operational tool: dense data tables, management workflows, and analytics that support repeated daily tasks. Separating routes and layouts is a usability pattern that reduces cognitive load and reduces the risk of exposing admin capabilities in customer contexts.

This separation also supports security in depth: even if a customer discovers an admin route, server-side authorization still prevents access, but the UI does not invite or encourage such navigation.

## 7. Implementation Details

This chapter links the report to the actual repository implementation.

### 7.1 Repository Structure

The project is split into `client/` and `server/`.

Client responsibilities:

- Routing and page composition
- Apollo client setup (HTTP + WebSocket split links)
- Form validation and UI components
- Admin screens and user screens

Server responsibilities:

- GraphQL schema and resolvers
- Controller functions implementing business rules
- Database models
- Middleware for permissions and error handling
- External service integration (Stripe, Cloudinary, email)

### 7.2 Authentication Module

Implementation summary:

- Password hashing: user model uses a pre-save hook to hash passwords with bcrypt.
- Login: server issues JWT and stores it in an HTTP-only cookie.
- Current user: GraphQL query returns authenticated user based on cookie context.
- Password reset: token generation, email dispatch, and token verification/expiry checks.

Security rationale:

- JWT cookie reduces exposure to XSS compared to localStorage tokens.
- Password reset tokens stored hashed reduce risk if DB leaked.

### 7.3 Room Module

Room listing:

- Supports searching and filters via an API filters utility.
- Uses pagination to keep listing efficient.

Room creation/update:

- Images are uploaded to Cloudinary.
- If an error occurs after upload, the implementation deletes uploaded images to avoid orphaned media.

Room details:

- Populates reviews and reviewer info for rich detail pages.

### 7.4 Booking Module

Booking overlap prevention:

- Before creating a booking, the server checks if another booking exists for the same room where dates overlap and status is not cancelled.

Amount calculation:

- Rent is calculated from rentPerDay and daysOfRent.
- Tax is applied as a percentage.
- Discounts are applied based on membership tier or referral code logic.

Cancellation:

- Cancellation is restricted by ownership or admin role.
- Non-admin users cannot cancel after check-in date.
- Card payments trigger refund via Stripe if payment is already paid.

#### 7.4.1 Overlap Detection Algorithm Explanation

Overlap detection is a correctness-critical rule in hotel systems. Two bookings overlap when their date ranges intersect. A standard interval overlap test is:

- existing.startDate < requested.endDate AND existing.endDate > requested.startDate

If both conditions hold, the bookings share at least one day in common (or one begins before the other ends). This test is efficient and avoids generating a list of dates for every booking. In this project, the server queries for an existing booking for the same room where status is not cancelled and the date overlap condition is true. If a record exists, booking creation is rejected with a clear error message.

This design directly addresses the core business risk of double booking and is safer than relying on client-side availability checks alone. Even if the UI shows a date as available, a second client can attempt a booking at the same time; server-side validation is the final authority.

#### 7.4.2 Discount and Referral Logic (Business Rules)

The system integrates discounts through membership tiers and referrals. The rules are designed to be simple and auditable:

1. If the user has an active membership tier, apply that tier’s discount percentage.
2. If the user does not have a membership tier, allow a referral code from an active member to grant a referral discount.
3. Do not stack membership and referral discounts; membership takes priority.
4. When a valid referral code is used, increment referral points for the referral owner.

These rules are implemented server-side to prevent manipulation. They also support business goals: membership encourages retention, referrals encourage acquisition, and the system ensures discounts are applied only under defined conditions.

### 7.5 Payment Module

Stripe checkout:

- Checkout session is created for room bookings and buffet bookings.
- The server provides success and cancel redirect URLs to the client.

Webhook confirmation:

- Webhook endpoint validates signed events.
- On checkout completion, the booking record is updated to confirmed and payment information is stored.

Why webhook confirmation matters:

- Client-side redirects are not sufficient proof of payment.
- The webhook is the reliable source of truth for successful payment.

#### 7.5.1 Payment State and Auditability

A payment integration should produce an auditable and unambiguous state for each booking. This project models payment and booking confirmation as a state progression:

1. Booking created: booking status is `pending`, payment status is `pending`.
2. Stripe checkout completed: a signed webhook event is received; the booking is updated with `paymentInfo` and status becomes `confirmed`.
3. Manual cash payment (where supported): payment method is set to `cash` and the booking is marked `confirmed` once staff confirm payment.
4. Cancellation: booking status becomes `cancelled` and cancellation time and reason are stored.
5. Refund (card): refund information is stored and payment status becomes `refunded`.

This state-driven approach supports staff workflows. Staff can answer questions such as “Is this booking confirmed?” and “Was payment completed?” without interpreting scattered ledger notes. It also supports reporting because booking status and payment status are explicit fields that can be aggregated.

#### 7.5.2 Why Server-Side Totals Matter

In payment systems, the amount charged must be consistent with the booking record. If the server trusted client-provided totals, an attacker could modify the payload and pay less than required. In this project, the server calculates rent, tax, discount, and total based on authoritative parameters (rent per day, days of rent, discount rules). This ensures the Stripe session uses a server-controlled total and reduces fraud risk.

### 7.6 Buffet Module

Buffet dinner creation:

- Validates time range (start < end).
- Validates included dishes against a dish catalog.

Buffet capacity management:

- Computes reserved seats across bookings (excluding cancelled).
- Prevents new bookings that exceed remaining capacity.

Buffet payment:

- Uses Stripe checkout and webhook confirmation similarly to room bookings.

### 7.7 Promotions and Membership Tiers

Promotions:

- Admin-only create/update/delete.
- Promotion codes normalized to uppercase and checked for duplicates.

Membership tiers:

- Tiers are constrained to defined names (silver/gold/diamond).
- Default tiers can be auto-seeded for consistent availability.

Referrals:

- Referral code validation confirms the owner is active and has a membership tier.
- Referral points are incremented for owners when a valid code is used.

### 7.8 Reviews

Eligibility:

- User can review a room only if they have a paid booking for that room.

Create/update behavior:

- If a review exists for the user+room, it is updated; otherwise it is created.

Admin moderation:

- Admin can delete reviews; deletion removes the review from the associated room’s review list.

### 7.9 Dashboard and Reporting

Reporting uses database aggregation:

- Sales grouped by date.
- Payment method distribution.
- Booking status distribution.
- Confirmed/cancelled counts.
- Average booking value and unique rooms booked.

Charts are presented in the admin dashboard UI.

#### 7.9.1 Dashboard Aggregation Design

The dashboard is designed to answer management questions quickly:

- How much revenue was generated in a given period?
- How many bookings were created, confirmed, or cancelled?
- How much revenue is still pending due to unpaid cash bookings?
- What payment methods do guests use most?
- Are cancellations increasing, and are they correlated with date ranges?

To support these questions efficiently, the backend uses MongoDB aggregation queries that compute multiple metrics in one request. This reduces repeated database queries and ensures the metrics are computed consistently from the same dataset. The implementation also normalizes date ranges to include full days (start at 00:00:00, end at 23:59:59) so that charts and totals align with human expectations for “daily” reporting.

An additional design detail is filling missing days in the sales chart with zeros. Without this, charts can appear irregular and can mislead users into interpreting missing data as “no data collected” rather than “zero activity.” Filling the time series produces continuous graphs that are easier to read.

### 9.6 Limitations and Validity Threats

2. Environmental variability: hotel demand is seasonal and influenced by external factors (tourism trends, economic conditions, local events). Comparing one period to another without controlling these factors can overstate or understate system impact.
3. User learning effects: staff efficiency typically improves after training and repeated use. Early measurements may reflect training gaps rather than system design quality, so evaluation should include both early-stage and post-adoption measurements.

5. Feature-scope boundaries: this system intentionally does not include full channel management, housekeeping scheduling, or multi-property support. Therefore, business comparisons to enterprise PMS products must account for scope differences and avoid unfair expectations.

By documenting these validity threats, the evaluation can be interpreted correctly: the system demonstrably improves workflow integrity and automation for core operations, while long-term business metrics should be confirmed through structured operational monitoring after deployment.

# Detailed Functionalities

This document explains the major functionalities of the Hotel Booking Management System, how they are implemented, why the implementation approach is useful, and what business value each feature brings.

## 1. User Authentication and Account Management

### What It Does

Users can register, log in, log out, update their profile, upload an avatar, change their password, and reset a forgotten password through email.

### How It Is Implemented

- The backend exposes authentication and profile operations through GraphQL user mutations and queries.
- Passwords are checked with `bcryptjs`.
- After login, the server creates a JWT and stores it in an HTTP-only cookie.
- The Apollo server reads the cookie on each request, validates the JWT, and attaches the authenticated user to the GraphQL context.
- Profile avatar uploads are sent to Cloudinary, and old avatar images are deleted when replaced.
- Password reset uses a generated reset token, stores the hashed token with an expiry time, sends a reset link by email, and validates the token before allowing a password update.

### Why This Is Implemented Well

- HTTP-only cookies reduce frontend exposure of authentication tokens.
- Central GraphQL context authentication keeps permission checks consistent across modules.
- Password reset tokens are hashed before storage, which is safer than storing raw reset tokens.
- Cloudinary cleanup prevents unused avatar files from accumulating.

### Business Value

- Customers can manage their own accounts, reducing staff workload.
- Secure login and password recovery improve customer trust.
- Profile management creates a more personalized customer experience.
- Admins can identify and manage customers more easily.

## 2. Role-Based Access Control

### What It Does

The system separates normal customer features from admin-only features. Admins can manage rooms, customers, bookings, promotions, buffets, membership tiers, reviews, and dashboard reports.

### How It Is Implemented

- Protected frontend routes use `ProtectPage`.
- Backend authorization is handled with `graphql-shield`.
- The permission layer checks whether the user is authenticated and whether the user's role includes `admin`.
- Sensitive GraphQL operations, such as room creation, promotion management, customer management, and dashboard data, require admin access.

### Why This Is Implemented Well

- Permissions are enforced on the backend, not only in the UI.
- Authorization rules are centralized in one middleware file, making them easier to audit and maintain.
- Frontend route protection improves user experience by hiding restricted screens from unauthorized users.

### Business Value

- Protects business data from unauthorized access.
- Reduces operational risk by limiting administrative actions to approved users.
- Supports real-world hotel workflows where staff and customers need different access levels.

## 3. Room Browsing and Room Management

### What It Does

Customers can browse rooms, search and filter available rooms, view room details, images, ratings, and reviews. Admins can create, update, delete, and manage room information.

### How It Is Implemented

- Room data is stored in MongoDB through a Mongoose room model.
- The GraphQL room API supports room listing, pagination, searching, filtering, and fetching details by ID.
- Admin room creation and updates support multiple image uploads to Cloudinary.
- When a room image is deleted, the system removes it from both Cloudinary and the room document.
- Room details populate related reviews and user information.

### Why This Is Implemented Well

- Pagination keeps room listing pages efficient as data grows.
- Search and filters improve discoverability.
- Cloudinary storage keeps image delivery separate from application storage.
- The backend cleans up uploaded images if room creation fails, preventing orphaned media.

### Business Value

- High-quality room listings help customers make booking decisions.
- Search and filtering reduce friction and increase conversion.
- Admins can keep inventory, pricing, and room information up to date without developer support.
- Better room presentation can directly increase booking revenue.

## 4. Room Booking

### What It Does

Authenticated customers can create room bookings by choosing dates, customer details, notes, and optional referral codes. Users can view their booking history and admins can manage all bookings.

### How It Is Implemented

- Booking creation is handled through GraphQL mutations.
- The backend checks for overlapping bookings before creating a new booking.
- Booked dates can be requested by room ID so the frontend can prevent users from selecting unavailable dates.
- Booking amounts are calculated on the backend using rent per day, number of days, tax, membership discount, and referral discount.
- User booking history includes metadata such as total bookings, unpaid bookings, and total unpaid amount.
- Admin booking lists populate room, user, and referral owner information.

### Why This Is Implemented Well

- Overlap detection protects room inventory from double booking.
- Server-side amount calculation is more reliable than trusting frontend totals.
- Returning booked dates improves the booking form experience.
- Metadata in the booking history helps users quickly understand pending actions.

### Business Value

- Prevents lost revenue and customer disputes caused by double bookings.
- Automates reservation processing.
- Gives customers self-service booking access at any time.
- Gives staff a centralized view of booking demand and payment status.

## 5. Payments and Payment Confirmation

### What It Does

Customers can pay for room bookings and buffet bookings online through Stripe. The system confirms bookings when Stripe payment succeeds.

### How It Is Implemented

- The backend creates Stripe Checkout sessions for room bookings and buffet bookings.
- Checkout sessions include customer email, product details, total amount, and metadata identifying the booking type.
- Stripe redirects customers to success or cancellation pages.
- A Stripe webhook listens for `checkout.session.completed`.
- When payment succeeds, the webhook updates the related room booking or buffet booking with payment information and sets the status to confirmed.

### Why This Is Implemented Well

- Stripe Checkout avoids building and storing sensitive card entry logic inside the app.
- Webhook-based confirmation is more reliable than relying only on frontend redirect status.
- Metadata allows one webhook handler to support multiple booking types.
- Payment information is stored on the booking record for later reporting and support.

### Business Value

- Enables immediate online payments.
- Reduces manual payment handling.
- Improves cash flow by confirming paid bookings automatically.
- Creates a clear payment audit trail for operations and accounting.

## 6. Booking Cancellation and Refund Support

### What It Does

Users can cancel eligible room bookings and buffet bookings. Room bookings paid by card can trigger refunds through Stripe.

### How It Is Implemented

- Cancellation checks whether the requester is either the booking owner or an admin.
- Room bookings cannot be cancelled by normal users after the check-in date.
- If a room booking was paid by card, the backend creates a Stripe refund.
- Cancelled bookings store cancellation time, reason, status, and refund information.
- Cancelled room bookings are excluded from overlap checks, making the dates available again.

### Why This Is Implemented Well

- Ownership checks prevent users from cancelling bookings that do not belong to them.
- Date validation protects business rules around check-in.
- Refund information is stored directly on the booking, making support and auditing easier.
- Excluding cancelled bookings from availability calculations keeps inventory accurate.

### Business Value

- Gives customers controlled self-service cancellation.
- Reduces support workload for common cancellation cases.
- Keeps booking availability accurate after cancellations.
- Creates a transparent record of refunds and cancellation reasons.

## 7. Buffet Dinner Management and Booking

### What It Does

Admins can create and manage buffet dinner events. Customers can browse available buffet dinners, view details, reserve seats, pay, and cancel bookings.

### How It Is Implemented

- Buffet dinners include title, cuisine category, description, image URL, start/end time, dishes, capacity, price, facilities, and active status.
- The backend validates that buffet end time is after start time.
- Included dishes are validated against the dish catalog and cuisine category.
- Available buffet dinners only include active future events.
- Seat capacity is calculated from existing non-cancelled bookings.
- Customers cannot book more seats than the remaining capacity.
- Buffet booking amount is calculated from price per guest plus tax.

### Why This Is Implemented Well

- Capacity validation prevents overbooking seats.
- Time validation prevents invalid event schedules.
- Dish validation keeps buffet data consistent.
- Active/future filtering prevents customers from booking expired events.
- Server-side price calculation keeps totals consistent and trustworthy.

### Business Value

- Adds an additional revenue stream beyond room bookings.
- Helps hotels promote dining events and packages.
- Prevents operational problems caused by overcapacity.
- Gives management better control over event availability and guest counts.

## 8. Promotions and Discounts

### What It Does

Admins can create and manage promotions with discount type, discount value, validity dates, active status, maximum uses, and usage count.

### How It Is Implemented

- Promotions are managed through GraphQL queries and mutations.
- Admin-only permissions protect promotion creation, updates, and deletion.
- Promotion records store validity windows, activity status, usage limits, and usage count.
- Promotion management screens allow staff to configure discounts without code changes.

### Why This Is Implemented Well

- Promotion data is structured, not hard-coded.
- Admin control enables fast business updates.
- Usage limits and valid dates support realistic campaign rules.
- Centralized promotion records make reporting and auditing easier.

### Business Value

- Supports marketing campaigns and seasonal offers.
- Helps increase occupancy during low-demand periods.
- Allows controlled discounting without losing operational oversight.
- Gives the business flexibility to respond quickly to demand changes.

## 9. Membership Tiers and Referral Rewards

### What It Does

The system supports membership tiers, tier-based discounts, referral codes, and referral points.

### How It Is Implemented

- Admins can create, update, activate, deactivate, and delete membership tiers.
- Membership tiers define discount percentages.
- Users assigned to a membership tier can receive automatic booking discounts.
- Members can have referral codes.
- Referral codes are normalized to uppercase before validation.
- Referral discounts apply to non-member users when a valid active member referral code is used.
- Referral owners receive referral points after successful referred booking creation.

### Why This Is Implemented Well

- Discounts are calculated on the backend from stored tier settings.
- Referral validation checks that the referral owner is active and has a membership tier.
- Referral logic encourages loyalty without allowing invalid codes.
- Admins can adjust tier benefits without changing code.

### Business Value

- Encourages repeat bookings through loyalty discounts.
- Turns existing customers into acquisition channels through referrals.
- Helps segment customers by value.
- Gives the hotel a practical loyalty program that can increase retention.

## 10. Reviews and Ratings

### What It Does

Customers can create or update reviews for rooms they have paid for. Admins can view and delete reviews.

### How It Is Implemented

- The backend checks whether the user has a paid booking for the room before allowing a review.
- A user can create a review or update an existing review for the same room.
- Reviews are linked to both the room and user.
- Room details populate reviews and reviewer information.
- Admins can delete reviews, and deleted review IDs are removed from the related room.

### Why This Is Implemented Well

- Paid-booking validation reduces fake reviews.
- Updating an existing review avoids duplicate reviews from the same user for the same room.
- Linking reviews to rooms supports rating display on room detail pages.
- Admin moderation protects content quality.

### Business Value

- Builds trust with future customers.
- Helps customers choose rooms confidently.
- Gives management feedback about room quality and service.
- Improves conversion by showing authentic guest experiences.

## 11. Admin Dashboard and Reporting

### What It Does

Admins can view business metrics such as sales, total bookings, pending amounts, paid cash, card sales, confirmed bookings, cancelled bookings, average booking value, booked room count, payment distribution, and booking status distribution.

### How It Is Implemented

- The backend uses MongoDB aggregation to calculate reporting data for a selected date range.
- Sales and booking counts are grouped by date.
- The system fills missing dates with zero values so charts remain continuous.
- Additional aggregate facets calculate payment totals, payment method distribution, status distribution, cancellations, confirmations, and unique booked rooms.
- The frontend displays metrics using dashboard cards and charts.

### Why This Is Implemented Well

- Aggregation happens in the database, which is efficient for reporting.
- Date normalization makes charts easier to read.
- Using one reporting query for multiple metrics reduces repeated database calls.
- Separating dashboard data behind admin permissions protects sensitive business information.

### Business Value

- Helps managers monitor revenue and booking trends.
- Supports better pricing, staffing, and marketing decisions.
- Makes unpaid or pending revenue visible.
- Helps identify cancellation patterns and payment preferences.

## 12. Customer Management

### What It Does

Admins can view customers, inspect customer details, update roles, activate or deactivate accounts, assign membership tiers, and adjust referral points.

### How It Is Implemented

- Customer management is exposed through admin-only GraphQL user queries and mutations.
- User updates support role changes, active status changes, membership tier assignment, and referral point adjustment.
- Referral point adjustment rejects negative changes in the current implementation.
- Member users without referral codes are automatically assigned codes when needed.

### Why This Is Implemented Well

- Admin operations are permission-protected.
- User status and roles are stored centrally on the user record.
- Membership and referral data are managed from the same customer module, keeping customer value data in one place.

### Business Value

- Lets staff manage customer lifecycle and access.
- Supports loyalty and VIP customer workflows.
- Allows the business to disable problematic or inactive accounts.
- Improves customer service by giving admins a full customer profile.

## 13. Invoice Generation

### Why This Is Implemented Well

### Business Value

- Gives customers payment and booking records.
- Reduces requests for manual receipts.

## 14. Tourist Attractions

### What It Does

The application includes a tourist attractions page that shows local destination information for guests.

### How It Is Implemented

- Attraction data is stored in frontend data files.
- The tourist attractions page is routed at `/attractions`.
- The feature is available from the user-facing layout and does not require backend calls.

### Why This Is Implemented Well

- Static local attraction data is simple, fast, and reliable.
- It avoids unnecessary backend complexity for content that does not need frequent updates.
- It enriches the guest experience without affecting booking workflows.

### Business Value

## 15. Real-Time Booking Notifications

### What It Does

The system supports GraphQL subscription notifications for new bookings and cancelled bookings.

### How It Is Implemented

- The server creates a WebSocket server on the same `/graphql` path.
- GraphQL subscriptions are enabled with `graphql-ws`.
- Booking creation publishes a `NEW_BOOKING` event.
- Room booking cancellation publishes a `BOOKING_CANCELLED` event.
- The Apollo Client is configured to split GraphQL operations between HTTP and WebSocket links.

### Why This Is Implemented Well

- Subscriptions are only used for real-time events, while normal queries and mutations remain on HTTP.
- The client uses Apollo split links, which is a standard pattern for GraphQL apps.
- Real-time updates can be added to admin workflows without polling.

### Business Value

- Staff can respond faster to new bookings and cancellations.
- Reduces the need for manual refreshes.
- Improves operational awareness during busy periods.

## 16. Data Validation and Error Handling

### What It Does

The application validates important business rules and handles errors consistently across backend modules.

### How It Is Implemented

- Controllers are wrapped with a shared `errorHandler`.
- Backend logic checks important rules such as room booking overlap, buffet capacity, buffet time validity, review eligibility, booking ownership, and password reset validity.
- Frontend forms use validation schemas in the `client/src/schema` directory.
- GraphQL returns structured errors for invalid operations.

### Why This Is Implemented Well

- Business rules are enforced on the backend, where users cannot bypass them.
- Shared error handling reduces repeated try/catch logic.
- Frontend validation improves usability before requests reach the server.
- Backend validation protects data integrity even if frontend validation is skipped.

### Business Value

- Reduces invalid bookings and operational mistakes.
- Improves user experience with clear validation feedback.
- Protects the quality and reliability of business data.

## 17. Seed Data and Development Support

### What It Does

The backend includes a seed script to populate initial data for development or demonstration.

### How It Is Implemented

- The server package includes `npm run db-seed`.
- Seeder files are stored in `server/seeder`.
- The script compiles and runs the seeder with TypeScript output in a temporary folder.

### Why This Is Implemented Well

- Developers and evaluators can quickly prepare a usable dataset.
- Seed data helps test the full workflow without manually creating every record.
- Keeping seed logic separate from runtime code avoids polluting production behavior.

### Business Value

- Speeds up development and testing.
- Makes demos more reliable.
- Helps new team members understand the system faster.

The system brings value by combining customer self-service, operational administration, online payment processing, loyalty management, dining event sales, and business reporting in one platform.

Key business benefits include:

- Increased booking conversion through searchable room listings and online reservations.
- Reduced manual workload through self-service booking, payment, cancellation, invoices, and profile management.
- Better revenue visibility through dashboards and payment tracking.
- More revenue channels through buffet dinner booking and promotional campaigns.
- Higher customer retention through membership tiers, referral rewards, and reviews.
- Lower operational risk through role-based access, backend validation, payment webhooks, and inventory checks.

## Summary

This project is more than a basic booking form. It models realistic hotel operations by connecting rooms, customers, bookings, payments, reviews, promotions, memberships, buffets, and reporting into a single full-stack system. The implementation uses strong separation between frontend and backend responsibilities, protects important business actions with permissions, and keeps critical calculations and validations on the server where they are reliable.
